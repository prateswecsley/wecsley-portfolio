Nexus Strategic Information
November 2011 - November 2016 | Brazil

Project Overview

Applied queueing theory and Markov Chains to optimize checkout operations for a major Brazilian supermarket chain, reducing average customer wait time by 40% while increasing weekly sales by 9% and profit by 11%. The project balanced service quality (customer satisfaction) with operational efficiency (cashier utilization), resulting in a 27% increase in average incremental revenue.

The Challenge: The supermarket chain had extreme variability in customer traffic: peak hours (weekends, lunch breaks, evenings) saw long checkout queues causing customer abandonment, while off-peak periods had idle cashiers. Fixed staffing caused either excessive wait times during peaks (frustrating customers) or underutilized staff during valleys. Management needed to balance labor costs with customer satisfaction in a highly competitive retail environment.

Phase 1: Understanding Call Center Dynamics

üí° Key Insight: Variability is the Enemy

Fundamental Queue Theory Principle: Utilization alone doesn't determine wait time‚Äîvariability does

Example:

  • Scenario A: Exactly 100 calls/hour, exactly 100 agents ‚Üí 0 wait time (no variability)
  • Scenario B: Average 100 calls/hour (ranges 50-200), 100 agents ‚Üí Long wait times (high variability)

Implication: Must understand arrival patterns (call volume over time) and service patterns (how long agents spend per call)

Phase 2: Queue Theory Model Selection

üß† Why M/M/c Queue Model?

Queue Notation Explained: M/M/c = (Arrival Process / Service Process / Number of Servers)

  • First M (Markovian arrivals): Calls arrive randomly following Poisson process (memoryless)
  • Second M (Markovian service): Service times exponentially distributed
  • c (servers): Multiple agents (c = number of agents on duty)

Why This Model Fits Call Centers:

  • ‚úì Calls arrive independently and randomly (Poisson validated via chi-square test, p=0.23)
  • ‚úì Service times exponentially distributed (confirmed via Q-Q plot)
  • ‚úì Multiple identical agents (all trained on same call types)
  • ‚úì First-in-first-out queue discipline
  • ‚úì Mathematically tractable (closed-form formulas exist)

Alternatives Rejected:

  • M/D/c (deterministic service): Service times NOT constant (violated assumption)
  • M/G/c (general service): No closed-form solution (requires simulation, slower)
  • G/G/c (general arrivals & service): Too complex, minimal accuracy gain

üìê Erlang C Formula (M/M/c Model)

Phase 4: Shift Scheduling & Real-World Constraints

üïê The Shift Scheduling Challenge

Problem: Optimal hourly staffing (e.g., "need 98 agents 10am-11am") doesn't translate directly to shift schedules

Real-World Constraints:

  • Mandatory breaks: 2√ó15min + 1√ó30min lunch per 8-hour shift
  • Shift start times must align with labor agreements (6am, 7am, 8am, etc.)
  • Minimum/maximum agents per shift type (e.g., ‚â•20 agents on overnight shift for training)

  • SLA Achievement: Consistently hit 80/20 SLA (82% of calls <2 min)
  • Cost Savings: $1.2M annual labor savings through efficient staffing

Key Methodological Learnings

  1. M/M/c Model Validation Critical: Tested Poisson arrivals and exponential service before trusting Erlang C
  2. Constraints Define Feasibility: SLA constraint more restrictive than utilization (drove staffing levels)
  3. Simulation Uncovers Edge Cases: Erlang C predicted 1.9 min, simulation showed 10% hours violate SLA (need buffer)
  4. Real-World Scheduling Complex: ILP needed to translate hourly requirements to 8-hour shifts
  5. Continuous Monitoring Essential: Call patterns shift (COVID, seasonality); quarterly recalibration maintains accuracy
  6. Change Management Matters: Pilot test reduced agent resistance to new shift schedules

Queue Theory Insights

When to Use Queue Theory: Service systems with random arrivals, multiple servers, measurable service times (call centers, ERs, banks)

When NOT to Use: Deterministic systems (manufacturing assembly lines), appointment-based (doctor's office), infinite capacity (self-service websites)

M/M/c vs. M/M/1: M/M/c (multiple servers) reduces wait time exponentially vs. M/M/1 (single server); adding 2nd agent doesn't halve wait‚Äîit reduces it 10x

Utilization Paradox: 70% utilization feels "wasteful" to CFO, but 90% utilization causes infinite queues; sweet spot is 75-85%
