üìà Marketing Mix Modeling (MMM)

M&T Bank
April 2024 - October 2024 | Buffalo, NY

Project Overview

Developed an econometric Marketing Mix Model for M&T Bank's $80M+ annual marketing budget, quantifying the ROI of 12 marketing channels (TV, Radio, Digital, Print, Direct Mail, etc.). The project combined time-series regression with advanced statistical techniques (adstock modeling, diminishing returns curves) to measure incremental impact and optimize budget allocation across channels.

The Challenge: M&T Bank's marketing team allocated $80M annually across 12 channels but lacked data-driven ROI measurement. Leadership couldn't answer: "How many accounts did our Q3 TV campaign generate?" or "Should we invest more in digital vs. traditional media?" Budget decisions were based on industry benchmarks and historical spending, not actual performance data.

Phase 1: Data Integration Strategy

üí° Critical Decision: What Level of Granularity?

Question: Should we model at daily, weekly, or monthly level?

Analysis:

  • Daily: 730 observations (2 years) - Too noisy (day-of-week effects dominate), marketing impact diluted
  • Monthly: 24 observations - Not enough data points for 30+ variables (overfitting risk)
  • Weekly (Chosen): 104 observations - Smooths noise while preserving sufficient sample size

Rationale: Marketing campaigns typically run 2-4 weeks; weekly aggregation captures campaign cycles without excess noise

üîÑ Data Collection Workflow

1
Marketing Spend Data (Independent Variables)
Source: Marketing finance system tracking all media spend Channels: TV (broadcast + cable), Radio, Digital Display, Paid Search, Social Media, Print, Direct Mail, Out-of-Home, Sponsorships Granularity: Daily spend by channel, aggregated to weekly Challenge: Inconsistent categorization (e.g., "Facebook Ads" vs "Social - FB") ‚Üí Created standardized taxonomy
2
Account Openings (Dependent Variable)
Source: Core banking system (CRM) Metric: New checking accounts opened per week (primary KPI) Why This Metric: Leadership cares about account acquisition (lifetime customer value = $1,200) Data Cleaning: Removed employee accounts, promo gaming, duplicate entries
3
External Control Variables
Economic: Unemployment rate, consumer confidence index (accounts seasonal trends) Competitive: Major competitor promotions (tracked via Ad Intel) Seasonal: Holiday indicators (Christmas, Tax Season drive account openings) Why Essential: Without controls, model might attribute seasonal uplift to marketing
4
Historical Campaign Calendar
Purpose: Validate if spend spikes align with known campaigns Example: Q4 2021 TV spike ‚Üí Confirmed with "Holiday Checking Promo" campaign Insight: Found $200K spend in weeks with no planned campaign (vendor billing error)

Phase 2: Advanced Feature Engineering

üß† Why Adstock & Saturation Transformations?

Problem: Raw marketing spend doesn't directly predict sales because:

  1. Carryover Effect: TV ad seen this week influences purchases next week (delayed impact)
  2. Diminishing Returns: First $100K in TV very effective; 10th $100K less so (saturation)

Solution: Transform raw spend with adstock (decay) and saturation (s-curve) functions

‚öôÔ∏è Feature Transformation Process

1
Adstock Transformation (Carryover Effect)
Concept: $100K TV spend in Week 1 has decreasing effect over subsequent weeks Formula: Adstocked_Spend[t] = Spend[t] + Œª √ó Adstocked_Spend[t-1] Parameter Œª (Decay Rate): 0.7 for TV (long memory), 0.3 for Digital (short memory) Why Different Rates: TV ads memorable for weeks; digital impressions forgotten quickly Calibration: Tested Œª from 0.1 to 0.9 in 0.1 increments, selected value with best model fit
2
Saturation Curves (Diminishing Returns)
Concept: First dollar of spend has higher ROI than millionth dollar Transformation: Hill function (S-curve) ‚Üí Saturation_Spend = Spend^Œ± / (K^Œ± + Spend^Œ±) Parameters: Œ± (shape), K (half-saturation point) Interpretation: When spend = K, you've captured 50% of max possible impact Calibration: Grid search over 100 (Œ±, K) combinations per channel Finding: TV saturates at $800K/week; Paid Search saturates at $50K/week (lower ceiling)
3
Lagged Variables (Delayed Response)
Question: Does marketing impact this week or next week? Approach: Created 1-4 week lags for each channel Finding: TV/Radio impact peaks at 2-week lag (awareness ‚Üí consideration ‚Üí action) Finding: Digital impacts same week (immediate response) Decision: Included optimal lag for each channel based on correlation analysis
4
Interaction Terms
Hypothesis: TV + Digital together may have synergistic effect Test: Created TV √ó Digital interaction variable Result: Coefficient positive and significant (p<0.05) Interpretation: $1 in TV worth more when also spending on digital (cross-channel reinforcement)

Phase 3: Model Development & Selection

üìâ Why Ridge Regression Over Other Methods?

Challenge: 30+ predictor variables (12 channels √ó adstock/saturation/lags + controls) with only 104 weekly observations ‚Üí High risk of multicollinearity and overfitting

Alternatives Considered:

  • OLS Regression: Unstable coefficients due to multicollinearity (VIF >10 for several variables)
  • Lasso Regression: Aggressively drops variables to zero (lost interpretability)
  • Random Forest: Black-box model (marketing team needs explainable coefficients)
  • Ridge Regression (Chosen): Shrinks coefficients without eliminating, handles multicollinearity, maintains interpretability

Key Advantage: Marketing VPs can say "TV has 2.3 ROI" (interpretable coefficient), not just "the model says TV is important"

üî¨ Model Training & Validation

1
Train-Test Split Strategy
Approach: Last 13 weeks (Q4 2022) held out as test set Rationale: Time-series data can't be randomly split (temporal dependence) Training: Weeks 1-91 (2020-2021 + most of 2022) Testing: Weeks 92-104 (Q4 2022 - includes holiday season)
2
Hyperparameter Tuning (Ridge Œ±)
Parameter: Œ± (regularization strength) controls shrinkage intensity Search Space: Tested Œ± from 0.001 to 100 (logarithmic scale) Method: 5-fold time-series cross-validation on training set Optimal Œ±: 2.5 (balanced bias-variance trade-off) Validation: R¬≤ = 0.87 on training set, R¬≤ = 0.82 on test set (good generalization)
3
Variable Importance Analysis
Method: Standardized regression coefficients (enables cross-channel comparison) Top Contributors: TV (Œ≤=0.45), Paid Search (Œ≤=0.32), Direct Mail (Œ≤=0.28) Surprising Finding: Print had near-zero coefficient (ineffective, candidate for budget cuts) Control Variables: Unemployment significant (higher unemployment ‚Üí more account openings to access credit)
4
Decomposition Analysis
Goal: Break down account openings into components (base + each channel + external) Baseline (No Marketing): 450 accounts/week (organic brand strength) TV Contribution: +180 accounts/week (40% of incremental) Total Marketing Contribution: +450 accounts/week (doubling baseline) Interpretation: Marketing drives 50% of total account openings

Phase 4: Budget Optimization & ROI Calculation

üí∞ ROI Calculation Methodology

Formula: ROI = (Incremental Revenue - Marketing Spend) / Marketing Spend

Incremental Revenue: Model-predicted accounts √ó $1,200 lifetime value per account

Example: TV drives 180 incremental accounts/week √ó $1,200 = $216K revenue vs. $90K spend ‚Üí ROI = 1.4 (140% return)

1

2

Radio: ROI = 1.7 (geographically targeted) Digital Display: ROI = 1.5 (retargeting effective) Social Media: ROI = 1.4 (engagement but lower conversion)
3

Print: ROI = 0.4 (losing $0.60 per $1 spent) Out-of-Home: ROI = 0.8 (billboard locations not optimized) Recommendation: Reallocate $2M from Print/OOH to Paid Search/Direct Mail
4
Saturation Point Analysis
Question: How much more can we invest before hitting diminishing returns? TV: Currently $90K/week, saturates at $800K/week ‚Üí Can 8x investment profitably Paid Search: Currently $45K/week, saturates at $50K/week ‚Üí Already near optimal Implication: TV has room to scale; Paid Search doesn't

üéØ Optimization Approach: Constrained Maximization

Objective: Maximize total account openings subject to:

  • Total budget = $80M/year (fixed)
  • Minimum spend per channel (can't go to zero for strategic reasons)
  • Maximum spend per channel (saturation limits)

Method: Nonlinear optimization (R optim() function with L-BFGS-B)

Result: Optimal allocation predicts 18% more account openings with same $80M budget

Key Business Outcomes

  • Budget Reallocation: Shifted $3M from low-ROI (Print/OOH) to high-ROI channels (Paid Search/Direct Mail)
  • ROI Transparency: First time marketing team had quantitative ROI for every channel
  • Strategic Decisions: Eliminated $2.5M print advertising based on model evidence (0.4 ROI)
  • Predicted Impact: 18% increase in account openings (1,800 additional accounts/year) with same budget
  • Cultural Shift: Moved from "gut feel" to data-driven budget planning
  • Scenario Planning: R Shiny app used in quarterly budget reviews by CMO

Key Statistical Learnings

  1. Adstock Critical: Model R¬≤ improved from 0.62 (raw spend) to 0.87 (adstock + saturation)
  2. Diminishing Returns Real: All channels showed saturation; linear models would overestimate impact
  3. Cross-Channel Synergy: TV √ó Digital interaction significant (15% lift when combined)
  4. Ridge > OLS: Ridge coefficients stable (low variance); OLS coefficients changed wildly with new data
  5. Weekly Granularity Optimal: Daily too noisy, monthly insufficient sample size
  6. External Controls Essential: Unemployment & seasonality explained 30% of variance (not marketing)

Methodological Insights

When to Use MMM: Established brands with 2+ years historical data, budget >$5M/year, multiple channels

When NOT to Use: Startups (no historical data), single-channel (no allocation problem), pure digital (use attribution models instead)

Biggest Pitfall: Confusing correlation with causality (unemployment correlates with accounts but not caused by marketing)
